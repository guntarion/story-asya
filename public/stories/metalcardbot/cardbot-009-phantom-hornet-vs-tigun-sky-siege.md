# Phantom Hornet vs. Tigun: The Sky Siege of Mowtown

## When Two Wild Cardbots Collide, Nobody's Lunch Is Safe

It started, as most catastrophes in Mowtown did, with a sandwich.

Specifically, it was *Edo's* sandwich. A beautiful, hand-crafted, triple-decker masterpiece with extra pickles and the last of his imported mustard. He'd been saving it for three days. He had written his name on the bag. He had drawn a skull and crossbones under his name. He had taped a note that read: **"TOUCH THIS AND I WILL DISASSEMBLE YOU FOR PARTS."**

None of this mattered, because Tigun ate it.

---

### Episode 1: The Tank Who Ate Everything

Tigun sat in the middle of Edo's garage, his green-and-white tiger-striped armor gleaming in the afternoon sun, his massive Growl Cannonaxes resting casually across his knees. He was licking mustard off his fingers — which shouldn't have been possible for a robot, and yet here they were.

"That," Tigun said, with the satisfied rumble of a tank engine after a full meal, "was an acceptable snack."

"SNACK?!" Edo's voice cracked in a frequency that made Blue Cop's sensors recalibrate. "That was my LUNCH! My DINNER! My EMOTIONAL SUPPORT SANDWICH!"

Tigun shrugged, which for a tank-based Cardbot meant his entire upper body shifted with a mechanical *CLUNK*. "You should have guarded it better. On Machina, if you can't protect your fuel, you don't deserve fuel."

"This isn't Machina! This is MY GARAGE! There are RULES!"

"Rules are for bots without cannons." Tigun patted his chest-mounted tank turret affectionately. It whirred in agreement.

Jun tried to intervene. "Tigun, come on, you can't just eat people's—"

"I am a *tiger*," Tigun said, his green eyes narrowing with absolute seriousness. "Tigers do not apologize for hunting."

"It was a SANDWICH! In a BAG! In a FRIDGE!"

"A fridge is just a cage for food. I liberated it."

Edo looked like he was about to cry, combust, or both. Theo was furiously scribbling calculations on whether a Cardbot's energy conversion system could even process pickles. (Results: inconclusive but terrifying.)

From the shadows of the garage rafters, a pair of hexagonal green eyes watched the entire scene with quiet disdain.

---

### Episode 2: The Ghost in the Rafters

Phantom Hornet had been sitting in those rafters for six hours.

Nobody had noticed him. This was exactly how he liked it.

The burgundy shinobi Cardbot was crouched on a support beam with the stillness of a gargoyle, his propellers folded flat against his back, his claw-fingers wrapped around the beam without making a single sound. He had been watching the garage's daily chaos unfold below him like a nature documentary about particularly noisy animals.

Tigun's sandwich crime did not interest him. Edo's wailing did not interest him. Jun's peacemaking did not interest him.

What interested Phantom Hornet was *silence*. And there was absolutely none of it here.

"I regret every decision that led me to this planet," he muttered, barely above a whisper.

He had been sealed by Soleil three weeks ago. Since then, she had insisted he "integrate" with the Mowtown Cardbots. Learn to coexist. Be a *team player*.

Phantom Hornet would rather eat his own Buzz Sting.

He was about to leave — slip through the ventilation shaft he'd mapped on day one, escape to the mountain cave he'd been using as a meditation spot — when Tigun looked up.

Directly at him.

"Hey," Tigun called out, his voice carrying the casual weight of a bowling ball thrown through a window. "Creepy helicopter guy. Want to spar?"

Every head in the garage turned upward.

"There's someone UP THERE?!" Anna yelped.

"How long has he BEEN there?!" Jun gasped.

Phantom Hornet closed his eyes. Took a breath. Reminded himself that Soleil had specifically said "no vanishing for more than twelve hours at a time."

"No," he said.

"Why not?"

"Because I do not spar. I *eliminate*."

Tigun grinned — a wide, sharp, tigerish grin. "Even better."

---

### Episode 3: The Challenge

Within ten minutes, the entire population of Mowtown had somehow found out that two Wild Cardbots were about to throw down. This was mostly Blue Cop's fault.

"ATTENTION ALL CITIZENS," Blue Cop had broadcast on every frequency available, because Blue Cop treated every situation like a traffic emergency. "UNAUTHORIZED COMBAT ZONE FORMING AT EDO MOTORS. ALL NON-COMBATANTS EVACUATE. ALSO, I AM PLACING BETS. TWENTY UNITS OF MACHINASIUM ON THE TANK."

"You can't BET on fights!" Anna protested.

"I am an officer of the law. I can do what I want."

"That's... literally the opposite of how law works."

Mega Trucker had set up bleachers. Flash Vector was selling popcorn at speeds that defied thermodynamics. Crest was reading a book and pretending he didn't care, but he'd strategically positioned himself with an unobstructed view. Even Buffalo Crush had shown up, somehow wearing a foam finger that read "#1 VIOLENCE FAN."

In the cleared-out center of the garage, Tigun stood with his Growl Cannonaxes already drawn — two massive handheld axes with snarling tiger faces embedded in the blades. The turret on his chest rotated slowly, like a predator scanning for prey.

Opposite him, Phantom Hornet had finally descended from the rafters. He stood perfectly still, his Buzz Sting gauntlet humming on his left arm, the kunai-style stinger glinting. His propellers twitched once. His hexagonal eyes were unreadable.

"Rules?" Jun asked desperately, trying to maintain some order.

"There are no rules in a Wild Cardbot duel," Tigun said.

"I agree," Phantom Hornet said. "No rules."

"Great, so at least don't destroy the—"

**BOOM.**

Tigun fired his chest cannons. The entire back wall of the garage ceased to exist.

"—garage." Jun finished, his hair blown sideways by the shockwave.

Edo fainted.

---

### Episode 4: Sky vs. Ground — The Worst Game of Tag

The fight that followed was, by unanimous agreement, the most chaotic thing Mowtown had ever witnessed — and this was a town where a pirate monster truck once crashed through a school during a parent-teacher conference.

Tigun fought like what he was: a TANK. He planted his feet, braced his enormous frame, and unleashed everything in his arsenal. His Growl Cannonaxes swung in devastating arcs that carved trenches in the asphalt. His chest turret fired rounds that detonated with concussive force, sending parked cars tumbling like dominos.

But you can't hit what you can't catch.

Phantom Hornet was *everywhere* and *nowhere*. His propellers buzzed to life and he shot into the sky, weaving between cannon blasts with the fluid grace of, well, a hornet. He fired volleys of energy kunai that rained down on Tigun's position, each one detonating in small flashes of purple light.

"Hold STILL!" Tigun roared, swinging an axe that sliced through nothing but air and a particularly unlucky mailbox.

"An assassin who holds still is a dead assassin," Phantom Hornet replied from somewhere above, his voice coming from at least three directions at once.

**PHANTOM-LEGION!**

Suddenly there were *six* Phantom Hornets in the sky — holographic clones that scattered in every direction. They all dove at Tigun simultaneously, Buzz Stings extended.

Tigun's response was elegant in its simplicity.

He aimed his chest turret straight up and fired everything.

**EVERYTHING.**

The resulting explosion lit up the Mowtown skyline like a premature New Year's celebration. Five holographic Phantom Hornets dissolved. The real one barely dodged, spiraling sideways with a singed propeller blade.

"Not bad," Phantom Hornet admitted, which for him was practically a standing ovation.

"I'm a TANK," Tigun said, cracking his neck. "We don't do 'bad.'"

From the sidelines, Theo was having the time of his life. "The tactical matchup is FASCINATING! Tigun has overwhelming firepower but limited vertical mobility! Phantom Hornet has superior agility and stealth but lower direct damage output! It's the classic ground-superiority versus air-superiority military doctrine!"

"NERD!" Tigun and Phantom Hornet shouted simultaneously.

Then they looked at each other.

Then they immediately went back to trying to destroy each other.

---

### Episode 5: The Real Enemy Arrives

The battle had been raging for twenty minutes. Half the commercial district was on fire (Mega Trucker was handling that). Three traffic lights had been decapitated (Blue Cop was having a crisis). And a flock of pigeons had permanently relocated to the next city.

Phantom Hornet was perched on a water tower, breathing hard. One propeller was damaged. His Buzz Sting had a crack running down the stinger.

Tigun stood in the street below, one Cannonaxe driven into the asphalt for support. His armor was scored with kunai impacts. His chest turret was overheated and steaming.

Both Wild Cardbots were exhausted. And neither was willing to admit it.

"Surrender," Phantom Hornet said.

"You first," Tigun growled.

"I was here first."

"That's not how surrendering works."

"I don't know how surrendering works. I've never done it."

"Me neither! So we're STUCK!"

And that's when the ground shook.

Not from their fight. Something *else*. Something bigger.

From the forest at the edge of Mowtown, trees began falling. A massive shape pushed through the treeline — a Cardbot, but *wrong*. Its body was covered in pulsing purple veins. Its eyes were wild, feral, burning with a sickly violet light. Mechanical fur bristled across its frame. Its jaws — actual, biological-looking JAWS — snapped open and released a shriek that made every window in a three-block radius shatter.

"Beast Virus," Phantom Hornet breathed, and for the first time, his voice carried something other than cold detachment.

It carried *fear*.

The infected Cardbot — some unknown Wild bot that had never been sealed — charged into Mowtown with the mindless fury of a hurricane. It smashed through buildings like cardboard, heading straight for the populated areas.

Straight for Jun, Theo, Anna, and the others.

"EVERYONE MOVE!" Jun screamed.

Tigun moved first. Not away. *Toward* it.

He planted himself directly in the beast's path, axes raised, turret already spinning up. "HEY! UGLY! Over here!"

The beast slammed into Tigun with the force of a freight train. Tigun's feet carved twin furrows in the street as he was driven backward, his axes locked against the creature's jaws. Sparks flew. Metal screamed.

"Little help would be GREAT!" Tigun grunted, his servos straining.

Phantom Hornet was already moving.

---

### Episode 6: The Assassin and the Tank

The funny thing about two fighters who've just spent twenty minutes learning each other's every move is that they make a terrifyingly effective team.

Phantom Hornet dropped from the sky like a burgundy meteor, his Buzz Sting spinning at full drill speed.

**STING RAID!**

A flurry of precise fencing strikes hit the beast's exposed back joints — exactly the weak points Phantom Hornet had been calculating since the creature emerged. The beast shrieked, its grip on Tigun loosening for just a second.

That was all Tigun needed.

**GROWL CANNONAXE — DOUBLE STRIKE!**

Both axes swung in a perfect X-pattern, catching the beast across the chest and sending it staggering backward. Tigun didn't let up — he converted his chest turret and fired a point-blank barrage that drove the creature into the town square.

"Pin it down!" Phantom Hornet called from above, and there was something new in his voice — not a command from an assassin, but a request from a *partner*.

"On it!" Tigun braced and swung both axes into the ground, creating a shockwave that cracked the pavement beneath the beast, trapping its legs in broken asphalt.

Phantom Hornet activated Phantom-Legion again — but this time, instead of attacking, all six copies surrounded the beast in a perfect hexagonal formation, disorienting it with buzzing propellers and flashing afterimages.

The real Phantom Hornet rose above them all, his Buzz Sting glowing with concentrated energy. The kunai blade extended to its maximum length, humming with power.

**INFERNAL STINGER!**

A massive silhouette of a hornet formed around Phantom Hornet as he dove. The impact was surgical — hitting the beast's core processor, the source of the Beast Virus infection. The creature convulsed, the purple veins flickering.

"NOW, Jun!" Phantom Hornet shouted.

Jun was already running, Metal Breath raised. "METAL CARD — SEAL!"

A beam of light engulfed the infected Cardbot. It shrieked one final time, then collapsed, shrinking into a Metal Card that clattered to the ground. The purple glow faded. The Beast Virus was contained.

Mowtown went silent.

Then Buffalo Crush raised his foam finger and shouted: "ENCORE! ENCORE!"

---

### Episode 7: Two Kinds of Strong

The sun was setting over the wreckage of Mowtown's commercial district. Edo was calculating repair costs on a calculator that kept running out of digits. Blue Cop was directing traffic around a crater. Mega Trucker was composing a ballad about the battle. ("Two Wild hearts, one Wild sky / Tiger roared and Hornet learned to fly...")

Tigun sat on an overturned dumpster, holding one of his Cannonaxes up to the light to inspect a new crack. Phantom Hornet was perched on the roof above him, legs dangling over the edge — the closest to "relaxed" anyone had ever seen him.

"You're fast," Tigun said.

"You're loud," Phantom Hornet replied.

"That's not a compliment."

"It wasn't meant to be."

Silence. Then — impossibly — Tigun laughed. A deep, rolling, engine-rumble laugh.

"You know what? I like you. You're weird and creepy and you sit in ceilings like a bat, but you fight like you MEAN it."

Phantom Hornet was quiet for a long moment. Then: "You are... acceptable. For a ground unit."

From Phantom Hornet, this was practically a declaration of undying friendship.

"I spent the whole fight trying to blast you out of the sky," Tigun continued, tossing a pebble into the crater. "Wasted all that firepower. But when the real threat showed up, your air support was the only reason my cannons could land. Turns out, the thing I couldn't hit was the thing I needed most."

Phantom Hornet looked down at his cracked Buzz Sting. "And I spent the fight avoiding your firepower. But without your ground assault to pin the beast, my precision strikes would have been meaningless. Speed without force is just... running away."

Jun had been eavesdropping — badly, because he was hiding behind a trash can that was approximately one-third his size.

"You guys could be an amazing team, you know," he offered.

Both Wild Cardbots turned to look at him with identical expressions of mild irritation.

"We are NOT a team," they said in unison.

Then they looked at each other.

"...We might be a team," Tigun amended.

"A temporary tactical alliance," Phantom Hornet corrected.

"Yeah. That. But cooler."

Soleil appeared in the garage doorway, arms crossed, the ghost of a smile on her face. She'd been watching the whole thing — the fight, the teamwork, the awkward bonding. She didn't say anything. She didn't need to. Some lessons teach themselves.

From the roof, Phantom Hornet's propellers hummed softly in the evening air. For the first time since landing on Earth, the Assassin of the Sky didn't feel like leaving.

And on the ground below, Tigun holstered his axes, leaned back, and grinned his tigerish grin at the stars.

"Same time tomorrow?" he asked.

A single energy kunai thudded into the dumpster two inches from his head.

"Don't push it," said the voice from the rooftop.

Tigun laughed all the way back to the garage.

---

## The Lesson

**The people who challenge us the most can become our greatest allies — but only if we stop trying to beat each other and start learning from each other.** Tigun and Phantom Hornet spent their energy trying to prove who was stronger, but when a real crisis hit, they discovered that their opposite strengths — raw power and precise agility — made them unstoppable *together*. The rival they couldn't defeat became the partner they couldn't do without.

**Remember:** It's natural to butt heads with people who are different from you — the loud friend and the quiet one, the planner and the improviser, the athlete and the artist. But those differences aren't weaknesses. They're puzzle pieces. The things that annoy you about someone might be exactly what you need when it really matters. So next time you clash with someone, ask yourself: are we fighting each other, or could we be fighting *together*?
