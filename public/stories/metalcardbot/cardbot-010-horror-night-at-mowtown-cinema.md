# Horror Night at Mowtown Cinema

## Three Cardbots, One Movie, and Zero Chill

It was supposed to be a relaxing Friday evening.

Glober had been planning this outing for *weeks*. Ever since he'd discovered the concept of "movie theaters" during one of his nature road trips (he'd accidentally driven through a drive-in showing a nature documentary and was ENCHANTED), he'd been determined to give his two oldest friends the full Earth cinema experience.

"Trust me," Glober said, adjusting his safari hat with the enthusiasm of a tour guide about to reveal the Grand Canyon. "Humans sit in dark rooms, eat popped corn, and stare at a giant glowing wall for two hours. It's AMAZING."

Rock Crush stood behind him, hunched over, his mining helmet pulled low over his green visor. His Megalo Drill halves were folded tight against his arms like a security blanket. Every passing car made him flinch.

"I don't like dark rooms," Rock Crush muttered. "I don't like crowds. I don't like outside. Can we go back underground?"

"You've been underground for THREE MONTHS, buddy. You need sunlight. You need socialization. You need *popcorn*."

"What's popcorn?"

"Tiny explosions you eat. You'll love it."

Rock Crush did not look like he would love it.

And then there was Deep Bite.

The ultramarine-blue shark Cardbot was walking beside them with the casual swagger of someone who had absolutely zero awareness of how terrifying he looked. His dorsal fin caught the streetlight. His claw-fingers clicked against each other. His yellow eyes gleamed in the dusk. His jawline faceplate made every expression look like he was about to devour something.

A woman walking her dog crossed the street when she saw him. The dog didn't — it just fainted.

"What a lovely evening!" Deep Bite said cheerfully, completely oblivious. "The air smells like... what is that wonderful scent?"

"Fear," Rock Crush said flatly. "That's the smell of fear. From that lady. Because of your face."

"My face? What's wrong with my face?"

"Nothing," Glober said quickly, steering them toward the cinema. "Your face is great. Very... unique. Very expressive."

"It expresses HUNGER," Rock Crush said.

"It does NOT!" Deep Bite protested, accidentally flashing his metal teeth, which caught the light and gleamed like a row of serrated knives. A nearby teenager screamed and dropped his phone.

"...Okay, maybe a little bit," Deep Bite admitted.

---

### Episode 1: Three Tickets to Terror

The Mowtown Megaplex Cinema was a modest building with a flickering neon sign, a popcorn machine that hadn't been cleaned since the Speranza crashed four thousand years ago, and exactly one bored teenager working the ticket counter.

That teenager's name was Kevin. Kevin was about to have the worst shift of his life.

"Three tickets, please!" Glober announced, slapping the counter with his munsell-yellow hand. He had practiced this. He'd watched humans buy things. He even had Earth money — Jun had given him some after he'd tried to pay for gas with a Machina energy crystal and accidentally supercharged a gas station.

Kevin looked up from his phone.

Kevin looked at Glober — a bright yellow robot in a safari hat.

Kevin looked at Rock Crush — an indigo robot with drills for hands, trembling like a wet cat.

Kevin looked at Deep Bite — a shark-faced robot whose teeth extended past his jawline, whose dorsal fin scraped the ceiling, and who was currently waving at Kevin with clawed fingers and a smile that looked like the opening scene of a nature documentary about apex predators.

"Hi!" Deep Bite said warmly.

Kevin's soul briefly left his body.

"...Three tickets," Kevin whispered. "To what?"

Glober scanned the movie listings with the concentrated focus of an explorer mapping uncharted territory. His eyes landed on one title and lit up.

"THAT one!" He pointed at the poster. "'The Abyss Below: Revenge of the Deep.' It says it's about the ocean! Deep Bite, you love the ocean!"

Deep Bite's eyes sparkled. "THE OCEAN! On a GIANT SCREEN! Yes, please!"

Rock Crush squinted at the poster. It showed a dark underwater cavern, glowing red eyes in the darkness, and the tagline: *"THEY SWIM. THEY HUNT. YOU SCREAM."*

"Glober," Rock Crush said slowly. "I don't think this is a nature documentary."

"Nonsense! It has water in it! Water is nature!"

"The poster has BLOOD on it."

"That's... red water. Very natural. Volcanic minerals."

"It literally says 'YOU SCREAM.'"

"Excitement screaming! Like a roller coaster!"

Kevin handed them three tickets with the expression of a man who had accepted that reality was no longer functioning properly. "Theater 4. Down the hall. Please don't eat anyone."

"Why would we eat anyone?" Deep Bite asked, genuinely confused.

Kevin just pointed down the hall with a trembling finger.

---

### Episode 2: The Popcorn Incident

Glober insisted on the full experience. This meant popcorn.

The popcorn machine at the Mowtown Megaplex was an ancient device that rattled, popped, and occasionally emitted sounds that suggested it was haunted. Glober approached it with the reverence of an archaeologist uncovering a sacred artifact.

"Three large popcorns!" he declared to the concession stand worker, a girl named Sarah who was already texting her friends that "three robots just walked in and I think one is a shark."

Rock Crush was standing as far from the other customers as possible, pressed into a corner, his visor glowing green as he scanned the room for threats. "Too many people," he whispered. "Too loud. The popping machine sounds like gunfire."

"It's popcorn, Rock. It's literally just corn having a small party."

"I DON'T TRUST CORN."

Meanwhile, Deep Bite had discovered the butter dispenser.

"Glober," he called, his voice carrying the wonder of someone who'd just discovered fire. "Glober, this machine dispenses LIQUID GOLD."

"That's butter, Deep—"

But Deep Bite had already pressed the button. And held it. And kept holding it.

Butter cascaded out of the dispenser like a golden waterfall. It overflowed the popcorn bucket. It pooled on the counter. It began dripping onto the floor. Deep Bite watched it with the pure, unfiltered joy of a Cardbot who had spent centuries in the ocean and had never encountered dairy products.

"It just KEEPS COMING!" Deep Bite marveled. "Is this what treasure feels like?"

"DEEP BITE, LET GO OF THE BUTTON!"

"But it's so BEAUTIFUL!"

Sarah was filming everything for TikTok. Her caption read: "shark robot floods cinema with butter, more at 11."

By the time Glober wrestled Deep Bite away from the butter dispenser, they had:
- One bucket of popcorn that was 90% butter and 10% structural integrity
- One bucket that Rock Crush was holding at arm's length like a bomb
- One bucket that Deep Bite had accidentally crushed with his claw-fingers, creating a popcorn explosion that rained kernels across the lobby

"Full Earth experience achieved!" Glober said brightly, wiping butter off his compass shoulder marking.

"We're banned from the concession stand, aren't we?" Rock Crush asked.

"Only temporarily! Sarah said she'd unban us if the video gets a million views!"

---

### Episode 3: Lights Out, Panic On

Theater 4 was dark.

This was, in retrospect, a problem.

Rock Crush froze at the doorway. His visor switched to infrared, painting the theater in ghostly green. He could see rows of seats filled with humans — all potential threats. The ceiling was low. The exits were... he counted... two. Not enough. Definitely not enough.

"I can't do this," Rock Crush said, his voice tight. "It's dark. It's enclosed. It's like the tunnel under Mowtown where I got electrocuted. I can't—"

Glober was there instantly.

Not with a speech. Not with a pep talk. Just *there*, standing beside his oldest friend, one hand resting gently on Rock Crush's crimson shoulder. His green eyes glowed softly in the dark — not scanning, not alert, just... present.

"Hey," Glober said quietly. "I'm right here. Same as on Machina. Same as in the escape pods. You don't have to be brave. You just have to sit next to me."

Rock Crush's trembling slowed. Not stopped — slowed.

"...You'll sit on my left?"

"Always on your left. So your good visor side can see the screen."

"...Okay."

They found seats in the back row — Rock Crush's non-negotiable requirement ("Maximum distance from strangers. Minimum distance from the exit."). Glober sat on his left. Deep Bite sat on his right.

This created a new problem: the humans sitting nearby.

The family in the row ahead turned around to get comfortable and came face-to-face with Deep Bite's shark grin hovering directly behind them in the darkness. His yellow eyes glowed. His dorsal fin cast a shadow across three seats.

The father made a sound that could only be described as a "reverse hiccup."

The mother grabbed her children.

The eight-year-old pointed and yelled: "COOL! A SHARK MAN!"

"Thank you!" Deep Bite whispered happily. "I like your hat!"

The family relocated to the front row. The eight-year-old had to be carried away, not from fear, but because he wanted to stay and "pet the shark man."

"I think I'm making friends!" Deep Bite told Glober.

"You're doing great, buddy."

---

### Episode 4: The Movie Begins (And So Does the Chaos)

The lights dimmed fully. The screen flickered to life. Ominous orchestral music swelled through the speakers.

*"THE ABYSS BELOW: REVENGE OF THE DEEP"*

The opening scene: a dark ocean. Bubbles rising. Something moving in the depths — a shadow, enormous and hungry, circling a submarine.

Rock Crush's drill halves clenched against the armrests. The armrests crumpled like aluminum foil.

"It's just a movie," Glober whispered. "It's lights on a wall. Not real."

"The submarine looks real."

"It's CGI. Humans make fake pictures with computers."

"Why would humans make FAKE scary things?! They have REAL scary things! Like taxes! And traffic!"

On screen, the creature lunged at the submarine. A jumpscare — accompanied by a deafening orchestral STING — blasted through the speakers.

Rock Crush yelped, activated his Freezer Chest on pure instinct, and fired an ice beam directly at the screen.

A perfect circle of frost bloomed across the bottom-left corner of the screen, freezing the monster's tail in place.

The entire theater gasped.

"DID THE MOVIE JUST FREEZE?" someone shouted.

"IT'S 4D!" another person yelled. "I TOLD you the Megaplex was upgrading!"

"That is NOT 4D!" Kevin shouted from the back, where he was stress-eating Skittles. "SOMEONE IS SHOOTING ICE AT MY SCREEN!"

Glober grabbed Rock Crush's arms and gently pushed the Freezer Chest downward. "Easy, easy! We talked about the instinct-freezing thing!"

"I PANICKED!"

"I know, buddy. But we can't freeze the movie every time something scary happens."

"Why NOT? If I freeze it, it can't move. If it can't move, it can't be scary. Problem SOLVED."

The logic was, unfortunately, airtight.

Meanwhile, Deep Bite was watching the movie with an expression of growing confusion.

"That's not how submarines work," he said, tilting his head. "The hull pressure ratios are completely wrong. And that creature — is that supposed to be a shark? Its fins are backwards. And its teeth grow INWARD? That's a dental nightmare."

"Deep Bite, it's fiction—"

"Also, it's swimming in the WRONG DIRECTION for that current. Any real sea creature would know to use the thermocline drift for ambush efficiency. This monster is an AMATEUR."

"You're... critiquing the monster's swimming technique?"

"Someone HAS to! This is an insult to aquatic predators everywhere!"

---

### Episode 5: Deep Bite vs. The Horror Movie

As the movie continued, a pattern emerged.

Rock Crush was terrified of everything. Every dark corridor, every creaking sound, every sudden shadow sent his systems into fight-or-flight — heavy emphasis on "flight" with occasional bursts of "freeze everything in sight."

Glober was the anchor. Every time Rock Crush tensed, Glober's hand was on his shoulder. Every time Rock Crush's visor flared with panic, Glober whispered calm reminders. "Not real. Just lights. I'm here. You're safe."

And Deep Bite?

Deep Bite had become the movie's most aggressive film critic.

"Oh, COME ON!" Deep Bite said, loud enough for three rows to hear, as the on-screen monster chased sailors through a flooding corridor. "If that creature is semi-aquatic, it would NEVER chase prey on land! It would flood the entire lower deck first and hunt them in the water! Basic marine predator strategy! I learned this when I was THREE!"

"Deep Bite, shh—"

"And the humans! Why are they RUNNING? They have a submarine! Dive deeper! The creature's bioluminescence suggests it's adapted for shallow depths! Go below two hundred meters and it literally CANNOT follow! THIS IS BASIC OCEANOGRAPHY!"

An old man three rows ahead turned around. "Son, would you please be quiet?"

Deep Bite looked at him with his glowing yellow shark eyes.

The old man turned back around and did not speak again for the rest of the film.

The movie's climax arrived: the monster cornered the last survivors in an underwater cave. Darkness. Dripping. A slow, building musical score. The camera pushed in on a dark crevice where something was breathing...

Rock Crush was practically vibrating. His Megalo Drill halves were spinning involuntarily, creating a low *whirrrr* that the nearby audience mistook for surround sound effects.

"Glober," Rock Crush whispered, his voice cracking. "Glober, it's going to jump out. I can FEEL it."

"Close your visor if you need to. There's no shame in—"

**THE MONSTER EXPLODED OUT OF THE DARKNESS WITH A SHRIEK.**

The entire theater screamed.

Rock Crush screamed.

Rock Crush's Freezer Chest activated and shot an ice beam at the ceiling, creating a chandelier of icicles directly above the audience.

Glober dove across Rock Crush, wrapping both arms around him and physically pointing the Freezer Chest at the floor. "ROCK! ROCK! It's ME! You're okay! It's just a movie!"

And Deep Bite?

Deep Bite stood up in his seat.

All six-and-a-half feet of ultramarine-blue shark Cardbot rose in the darkness of Theater 4, his dorsal fin silhouetted against the screen, his yellow eyes blazing, his metal teeth flashing.

He pointed at the screen monster and announced, in a voice that carried across every row:

**"THAT SHARK HAS NO DORSAL LATERAL LINE SYSTEM! IT COULDN'T HUNT A SLEEPING ANCHOVY! I HAVE SEEN BETTER PREDATOR DESIGN ON A TOASTER! THIS IS SCIENTIFICALLY UNACCEPTABLE AND I DEMAND A REFUND!"**

Dead silence.

Then the eight-year-old from earlier, now sitting in the front row, stood on his chair and started a slow clap.

The entire theater joined in.

"YEAH!" someone shouted. "THE SHARK GUY IS RIGHT!"

"REFUND! REFUND!" the audience chanted.

Deep Bite bowed graciously, his dorsal fin sweeping through the projector beam and casting a perfect shark shadow across the screen that was, ironically, scarier than anything the movie had produced.

Kevin, watching from the door, put his head in his hands. "I don't get paid enough for this."

---

### Episode 6: After the Credits

They sat on the curb outside the cinema, bathed in the warm glow of the flickering neon sign. Rock Crush had finally stopped trembling. Glober's arm was still around his shoulder. Deep Bite was happily crunching the remains of their butter-soaked popcorn, which he had declared "the second-best thing about Earth after the ocean."

"I ruined the movie," Rock Crush said quietly, staring at his drill-hands. "I froze the screen. I froze the ceiling. I almost froze the audience. I can't even sit in a dark room without—"

"Without what?" Glober said gently. "Without reacting to something that scared you? Rock, the ENTIRE theater screamed at that jumpscare. Every single human in there was afraid."

"But they didn't shoot ICE at the ceiling!"

"No, they threw popcorn, screamed profanity, and one guy literally jumped into a stranger's lap. Humans are just as bad at handling fear — they just don't have freeze cannons."

Rock Crush almost smiled. Almost.

Deep Bite sat down on Rock Crush's other side, his large frame somehow managing to look gentle as he settled. "You know, Rock," he said, his voice softer now, "on Machina, everyone was afraid of ME. They called me the Outlaw of the Sea. They crossed to the other side of the corridor when I walked by. They assumed that because I looked scary, I WAS scary."

Rock Crush looked at him. "But you're not scary."

"No. I'm not. But I *acted* scary because I thought that's what everyone expected. It took Glober to show me that I could just... be me." He nudged Rock Crush's shoulder with his own. "And you don't have to be the tough underground loner, either. You're allowed to be scared. You're allowed to freeze a movie screen. You're allowed to need your friends."

"I froze the ceiling too."

"Honestly? The icicles looked really pretty. Several humans took photos."

Glober pulled both of them closer. "You know what the best part of tonight was? Not the movie. The movie was terrible."

"SCIENTIFICALLY terrible," Deep Bite added.

"The best part was that we did it together. For the first time since we left Machina, all three of us were in the same place, doing something normal. Something EARTH normal. And yeah, it was chaotic and weird and we're probably banned from this cinema forever—"

"Definitely banned," Rock Crush confirmed. "Kevin told me while I was apologizing for the icicles."

"—but we were together. That's what matters."

Rock Crush was quiet for a long time. Then he leaned slightly — just slightly — into Glober's side. For a bot who flinched at everything, this was enormous.

"Same time next week?" Rock Crush asked.

Glober beamed so hard his flashlight hat accidentally turned on. "REALLY?"

"But we're watching a nature documentary. A REAL one. With no jumpscares."

"Deal!"

Deep Bite raised a claw-finger. "Can I still critique the marine biology?"

"Only if you keep it under forty decibels."

"No promises."

They sat under the neon sign as Mowtown settled into its quiet evening routine. Three Cardbots from a dead planet, sharing popcorn crumbs and something far more valuable — the knowledge that you don't have to face scary things alone.

And inside the cinema, Kevin was explaining to his manager why the ceiling had icicles, the screen had a frost circle, the concession stand was flooded with butter, and the audience was chanting about a shark.

His manager just sighed and said: "Mowtown."

As if that explained everything.

It did.

## The Lesson

**Being brave doesn't mean not being afraid — it means letting the people who care about you help you through the fear.** Rock Crush spent months hiding underground, convinced that his fears made him weak. But Glober showed him that real friends don't judge you for being scared — they sit beside you and hold your hand through the dark parts. And Deep Bite proved that sometimes the scariest-looking person in the room is actually the kindest one there.

**Remember:** Everyone is afraid of something — horror movies, dark rooms, loud noises, new places, being judged. The difference between facing fear alone and facing it with friends is the difference between freezing up and getting through it. You don't need to pretend you're fine. You just need people who'll say "I'm right here" and mean it. And if the scary thing turns out to be a movie monster with terrible swimming technique? Well, it helps to have a friend who'll stand up and call it out!
