# Operation Takjil

## Sky, Speed, and a Very Stubborn Jet

The map on Gigantrex's display was not a battle map.

There were no enemy positions, no energy signatures, no threat vectors. Instead, it was covered in small green markers — each one a neighborhood, a village, a cluster of homes on the outskirts of Mowtown. Numbers hovered beside each marker: *estimated households*, *distance from commercial zone*, *last recorded access to community food distribution*.

Some of the numbers were small. Some of the distances were very large.

"This is my survey data," Gigantrex said. He stood in the middle of Edo Motors' main floor, his tanker trailer components settled behind him, his Rex Arm gauntlet pointing gently to the markers furthest from the city center. "I have been tracking Mowtown's geography for the past three weeks. These areas —" the gauntlet swept across the remote markers, "— have residents who cannot easily reach the city's bazaar. They have no transport. The roads are poor. Many of them are elderly, or families with young children."

He turned to face the assembled team.

"Ramadan ends in nine days. Every evening, these communities break their fast with whatever they can manage. Most of them have not had dates this year. Not once."

The room was quiet.

Tachy raised her hand. "How many areas?"

"Seven." Gigantrex pulled up the numbers. "Approximately four hundred households total."

"And we want to deliver takjil," Shadow X said. It wasn't a question — his tone was already calculating, already running logistics through his ninja-sharp mind.

"Iftar is at six-seventeen today," Gigantrex confirmed. "We have a window."

Shadow X straightened. "Then we run this as an operation. Organized. Coordinated." He looked at the team. His X-Wheel shuriken was folded at his back, but his posture radiated the focused energy of someone who had spent their entire existence on Machina practicing precision under pressure. "Tachy."

Tachy's cheetah armor practically vibrated. "Yes?"

"You're on collection. The halal food stores on Jalan Makmur, the date suppliers near the central market, the bakeries on the east side. You know this city faster than anyone. I need you to gather everything — dates, kolak packets, gorengan, es buah — and bring them to the loading point at the east garage. Prioritize the dates. Speed, but care. Nothing damaged."

Tachy was already bouncing on her heels. "Collection. Speed but care. Dates first. Got it. I am *born* for this."

"Sky Gallop handles transport." Shadow X looked up at the massive white Cardbot who stood apart from the group, his golden wing crests catching the garage lights, his chivalrous bearing making him look like a knight who happened to be the size of a building. "Your cargo capacity is the only thing that can cover seven remote areas in one run. Gigantrex will give you the route data directly."

Sky Gallop inclined his head — a graceful, deliberate motion. "It would be an honor," he said, and meant it completely.

---

That was when the sound of a Yak-130 fighter jet doing an unnecessary victory roll over Edo Motors announced Cielo's arrival.

He transformed mid-descent and landed beside the group with the casual confidence of someone who had not been invited but was absolutely certain they were welcome.

"I heard *operation*," Cielo said, his dual Nitro Express guns clicking into rest position. "I am in."

Shadow X looked at him. "You weren't briefed."

"I'm briefing myself. What are we doing?"

"Takjil delivery for Ramadan. Organized logistics. Seven remote areas."

Cielo's eyes lit up. "I fast now. I understand Ramadan. I am deeply invested in this cause." He paused. "Also I am extremely fast and I can fly, which seems relevant."

Shadow X considered him for a long moment with the expression of someone calculating risk versus resource.

"Fine," he said finally. "You're on aerial scouting. I need eyes on the road conditions between the east garage and the remote zones — weather, obstacles, anything that affects Sky Gallop's flight path. You report to me. You do not deviate. You do not improvise. You scout and you report. Clear?"

"Crystal," Cielo said cheerfully.

Shadow X looked at him for one more second.

"Crystal *clear*," Cielo confirmed. "Shadow X. Sir."

Shadow X turned back to the map. He did not look reassured.

---

The first two hours of Operation Takjil were a masterpiece of coordination.

Tachy exploded across Mowtown like a cheetah-colored comet. She hit the date suppliers first, negotiating with cheerful speed — the vendors had already heard about the community delivery, and most were offering supplies at cost or free entirely, proud to be part of something. Tachy piled crates of kurma onto the small flatbed cart she was towing, her sports car form weaving between traffic with surgical precision.

"Kolak packets next!" she announced into her comm. "Shadow X, the bakery on the east side says they have two hundred portions of gorengan ready. Do we want all of it?"

"All of it," Shadow X said. "Distribute evenly across the seven packages."

"On it." She was already accelerating.

Sky Gallop, parked at the east garage in his massive Boeing C-17 form, received each delivery from Tachy with calm efficiency. His cargo ramp was down, his hold organized into seven clearly marked sections — one per destination, labeled with Gigantrex's survey zone numbers. As Tachy delivered, Sky Gallop's loading system sorted each batch with graceful precision. Dates and water in every section first. Kolak and es buah next. Gorengan carefully sealed in heat-retention packets last.

"Zone Three is loaded," Sky Gallop reported. "Zone Five next."

"Confirmed," Shadow X said. "Tachy, Zone Five needs extra — Gigantrex's data shows the highest concentration of elderly households. Add one more kurma crate."

"Adding," Tachy confirmed, already pulling another crate from her flatbed.

Overhead, Cielo was doing his assigned scouting circuits. He checked road conditions along Sky Gallop's flight corridors, reported back weather (clear, light wind, sunset ETA confirmed at 6:17), flagged one section of road that had a downed tree branch, and sent a tidy summary to Shadow X every fifteen minutes.

It was going beautifully.

Shadow X allowed himself the rare sensation of things going according to plan.

This was a mistake.

---

The problem started at Hour Three.

Cielo was on his fourth scouting loop, skimming low over Zone Two's approach road, when he spotted something: a Pak Haris-style food cart had been set up at the edge of the zone, but the vendors looked overwhelmed. There was a small crowd — not hostile, just people who had heard something was coming, gathering early. Some of them had children. One elderly grandmother was sitting on a plastic chair she'd carried from her house.

Cielo saw the grandmother. He saw the children. He saw the dates that were *right there* in Sky Gallop's Zone Two hold.

He had a thought. The thought was: *I could just take Zone Two's allocation and deliver it right now. It's ready. People are waiting. Shadow X said Sky Gallop handles transport — but Sky Gallop is still loading Zone Six. I'm faster. This is efficient.*

The thought did not include: *checking with Shadow X first.*

He landed at the east garage, gave Sky Gallop a confident thumbs-up, grabbed the Zone Two cargo pallet with his aircraft landing gear hooks, and took off.

He delivered it to the crowd near Zone Two's edge road in under four minutes. The grandmother got her dates. The children clapped. Cielo felt excellent about himself.

He commed in. "Shadow X! Zone Two delivered. You're welcome!"

A pause.

"...you delivered Zone Two," Shadow X said.

"Ahead of schedule! Proactive operation." Cielo did a small celebratory loop. "I saw people waiting—"

"Cielo." Shadow X's voice was very quiet. "Zone Two was loading sequence four. Sky Gallop was going to fly Zones One, Three, and Five first — because those are the *farthest* from the city and the *least accessible*. Zone Two is fifteen minutes from the city by road. The families there have backup options. Zone Five is forty minutes out by air, has no road access, and the survey data shows they have *nothing*."

A longer pause.

"...oh," Cielo said.

"Zone Five's allocation was next to Zone Two in Sky Gallop's hold. When you grabbed the Zone Two pallet, you also displaced the Zone Five sorting. Sky Gallop had to re-sort. That cost us twenty-two minutes."

"I can fix—"

"It is four-fifty-eight PM," Shadow X said. "Iftar is at six-seventeen. Zone Five is forty minutes away by air. Sky Gallop still has Zones One, Three, Four, and Five to deliver. The math, Cielo, is *not comfortable*."

Cielo landed on the east garage roof and sat down.

He didn't say anything funny. For once, there wasn't anything funny to say.

---

Shadow X arrived on the roof two minutes later, his VTOL mode converting to robot form with practiced silence. He landed without a sound, which was impressive for a bot his size.

"Zone Two needed deliveries," Cielo started. "The grandmother—"

"I know about the grandmother," Shadow X said. "She was on Gigantrex's survey. She was in Zone Two. Zone Two's delivery was scheduled for *five forty-five*, after the priority zones were handled. She would have had her dates thirty-two minutes before iftar. Now Zone Five — which has no road access, no backup, and two hundred and forty people — may not get theirs at all, because our timing is gone."

"I was trying to help."

"I know."

"I wasn't trying to mess things up."

"I know that too." Shadow X sat down next to him, which was not a thing Shadow X typically did. Shadow X typically *stood*, with the contained energy of a coiled spring. But he sat. "Cielo. Your problem isn't that your heart was wrong. Your problem is that you thought one grandmother in front of you was more real than two hundred and forty people in a survey report."

Cielo's Nitro Express guns drooped slightly.

"They *are* real," Shadow X continued. "They're just not visible to you from here. That's why the coordinator exists. That's why Gigantrex made the survey. That's why I plan the sequence. Because the person in front of you always feels more urgent than the person you can't see — but in an operation, you have to trust the system to hold *everyone*, not just the person in your line of sight."

"I should have commed you first," Cielo said.

"Yes."

"Before touching anything."

"Yes."

Cielo looked at the horizon. The sun was already lower than he'd like.

"What do we do?"

"We fix it," Shadow X said, standing. "But you're going to follow my instructions exactly this time. No improvising. No 'I see something, I'll handle it.' You ask first. Every time."

"Every time," Cielo agreed.

---

They were interrupted by the sound of Gigantrex arriving.

The great tanker truck Cardbot moved with the unhurried certainty of someone who already knew the situation and had already begun thinking three steps ahead.

"I monitored the comms," he said. Not an accusation — a statement. He looked between Shadow X and Cielo. "How are we?"

"Cielo understands the mistake," Shadow X said.

"I do," Cielo confirmed. "I am genuinely sorry, not just sorry-because-I-got-caught sorry."

Gigantrex looked at him for a moment, with those ancient optics that had seen a colony ship cross dead space, had led a crew for four thousand years, had woken from stasis to a world utterly changed. He nodded once.

"Good," he said. "Then here is what happens now." He pulled up the survey map on his arm display. "Zone Five is forty minutes out. Sky Gallop cannot make it there and back and still complete Zones One and Three in time. But —" he highlighted two routes, "— if we split the remaining deliveries between Sky Gallop and a second aerial unit..."

He looked at Cielo.

Cielo sat up straighter. "I can carry cargo. My undercarriage has hook mounts."

"Zone Five's allocation is within your carry capacity," Gigantrex confirmed. "You fly direct to Zone Five. Straight line, no detours, no scenic routes. Shadow X coordinates you from here. You report every five minutes and you do what he tells you. Can you do that?"

"Yes," Cielo said. No jokes. No loops. Just yes.

Gigantrex turned to Shadow X. "And you — trust him to do it."

Shadow X met his captain's gaze. Breathed out slowly. "...yes, Captain."

"Cielo means well," Gigantrex said quietly, just to Shadow X. "His error today was not malice. It was inexperience with operations, and the difference between feeling helpful and *being* helpful. Correct the behavior. Keep the pilot." The ghost of a smile crossed his ancient face. "A jet who flies with his heart as well as his instruments is worth developing. Just... with more instruments."

Shadow X almost smiled back. Almost.

---

At five-seventeen PM, Cielo lifted off with Zone Five's allocation secured to his undercarriage: two hundred and forty portions of kurma, kolak packets, gorengan, and sealed es buah drinks, all carefully divided into individual household portions by Tachy, who had appeared out of nowhere at the garage and repacked everything in twelve furious, expert minutes.

"How did you do that so fast?" Cielo asked.

"I'm Tachy," she said, like it explained everything, because it did.

Sky Gallop launched simultaneously, carrying Zones One and Three in his vast C-17 hold, his enormous wingspan catching the late afternoon light as he banked westward. He looked, Cielo thought, exactly like his name — free and unhurried and impossibly large, a white knight crossing the sky.

"Zone Five," Shadow X said in Cielo's earpiece. "Heading two-seven-zero, then adjust to two-eight-five after the ridge. I'll give you the final approach. Report every five minutes."

"Two-seven-zero, adjusting to two-eight-five after the ridge," Cielo repeated. "Reporting every five minutes." A pause. "Shadow X?"

"What."

"I just want to say — you're really good at this. The coordination thing."

"Cielo."

"I mean it. Not being funny. You're genuinely good."

A shorter pause than expected.

"...fly the heading," Shadow X said.

Cielo flew the heading.

---

At six-oh-three PM, Cielo touched down in a wide field on the edge of Zone Five.

The area was small — a cluster of modest homes, a little mosque with a tiled roof painted pale green, vegetable gardens between the houses. People had gathered in the field, perhaps forty or fifty of them, having heard through some village network that something was coming. Children stood in a line that was more of a cluster. Several grandmothers sat in plastic chairs that had been carried outside.

Cielo set down the cargo as gently as a Yak-130 could set down anything, which was considerably more graceful than you'd expect from a machine that normally deployed dual machine guns as its primary feature.

A small girl walked up to him. She looked at his wings. She looked at his Nitro Express guns, folded politely at his sides. She looked at the crates.

"You brought the kurma?" she asked.

"Yes," Cielo said.

"Are you an angel?" she asked.

Cielo blinked. His optics cycled. He thought about making a joke — something about being a Cardbot from Planet Machina, about Yak-130 jets and Nitro Express machine guns and aerial combat capabilities, about how angels probably didn't have forward-facing weapon arrays.

He decided against all of it.

"Not quite," he said. "But I'm glad I'm here."

He helped them unload. The kurma went first — one portion per household, the same way Tachy had packed it. Then the kolak, the gorengan, the drinks. The village distributing officer organized it with the efficiency of long practice, and in twelve minutes, everything was sorted.

The sun was low and gold over the ridge.

"Five minutes," Shadow X said in his earpiece. "You made it."

"We made it," Cielo corrected.

A longer pause than usual.

"Yes," Shadow X said. "We made it."

---

At six-seventeen PM, the call to Maghrib prayer echoed across seven neighborhoods, from the mosque in Zone Five to the little surau in Zone One, from Pak Haris's cart near Edo Motors to Mrs. Laila's open window. The sound traveled across Mowtown and its outskirts like a river finding its way home.

Dates were the first thing eaten. As they always are.

In Zone Five, the small girl who had asked if Cielo was an angel sat with her grandmother and split a piece of kurma between them, very carefully, making sure both halves were exactly equal. Her grandmother said something in a language Cielo's translation systems didn't have, but the sound of it was clear: *thankful.*

Cielo sat in the field and watched the sun finish setting and thought about the difference between *feeling helpful* and *being helpful*, and how strange it was that the second one was so much harder than the first.

---

Back at Edo Motors, Tachy had somehow produced a plate of gorengan from somewhere and was sharing it with Sky Gallop, who sat in robot mode in the garage entrance, his golden wing crests gleaming in the last of the day's light.

"You're very large," Tachy observed, looking up at him.

"So I've been told," Sky Gallop said pleasantly.

"But graceful. For your size." She offered him a piece of gorengan. He held it between two enormous fingers and examined it with genuine curiosity.

"This is the food they call takjil?"

"Part of it. Fried stuff. It's good."

He tried it. His optics brightened with the same surprised pleasure that Fleta Z had once described when encountering methane — the Machinasium gustatory system encountering something it hadn't expected to enjoy.

"Remarkable," he said.

Shadow X landed on the garage roof in his VTOL form and converted to robot mode. He surveyed the team below — Tachy sharing gorengan with Sky Gallop, Cielo incoming on the horizon returning from Zone Five, Gigantrex standing by the door with his arm display still showing the completed mission markers, all seven zones now checked in green.

"Operation complete," he said, mostly to himself.

"Well done, coordinator," Gigantrex said from the doorway, without looking up from his display. He didn't say it often. He said it when he meant it.

Shadow X stood on the roof for a moment longer.

Below, Cielo touched down in the parking lot, shed his cargo hooks, and transformed to robot mode. He looked up at Shadow X on the roof.

"All households confirmed," he called up. "Zone Five is good. They said—" he paused, and something in his usually-playful optics was quieter than usual "— they said thank you. The actual people. They said it to my face."

Shadow X looked at him for a long moment.

"Next time," he said, "follow the brief."

"Next time," Cielo agreed, "I follow the brief."

He transformed back to jet mode, pulled a single small loop over the parking lot — restrained, celebratory, nothing that would knock over any food carts — and landed again neatly.

"But I'm glad I was there," he added.

"So am I," Shadow X said, and went inside before Cielo could see that he was smiling.

---

## The Lesson

**Good intentions need good execution.** Cielo's heart was absolutely in the right place — he saw someone who needed help and he acted. But an operation is a system built on trust, and when you break the chain by improvising alone, you don't just affect yourself. You affect every person that chain was built to serve.

**The people you can't see are just as real as the person in front of you.** Gigantrex's survey existed because someone sat down and thought about everyone — not just the visible crowd, but the grandmother in Zone Five who had no road, no cart, no backup. That kind of thinking is what coordinators are for.

**Remember:** When you're part of a team with a plan, your job isn't just to do good — it's to do your *part* of the good. Ask before you act. Trust the coordinator. And if you make a mistake, don't deflect with jokes — own it, learn it, and show up better for the next run. That's how one jet can help four hundred families have dates for iftar.

*Ramadan Mubarak!*
