# One More Video

## How a Single Click Nearly Broke Edo Motors

It started, as most disasters at Edo Motors do, with Buster Gallon having a *brilliant* idea.

"Mini Gallon Number Seven!" the green inventor-bot announced proudly, holding up a tiny drone that looked exactly like a miniature version of himself but wearing a small baseball cap. "I have equipped him with Wi-Fi connectivity and full internet browsing capability!"

Mini Gallon Number Seven blinked his tiny eyes, rotated once, and immediately opened a web browser.

Jun looked up from his homework. "Why does your little robot need Wi-Fi?"

"Research purposes," Buster Gallon said, in the exact tone of someone who definitely did not plan to use it for research purposes.

"There's a website," the green bot continued, fingers tapping together like a scheming villain in a cartoon, "called YouTube. I have been studying it. It contains approximately *800 million videos*."

Silence fell over the garage.

Buffalo Crush slowly raised his hand. "Does it have food videos?"

---

Within twenty minutes, every available screen in Edo Motors was streaming a different YouTube channel.

Buster Gallon had commandeered Edo's laptop and was watching a channel called "ENGINEERING FAILS COMPILATION #47" while taking frantic notes. Every time something exploded on screen, he'd lean forward and whisper, "Fascinating. Fascinating. I would have done it *much* worse."

Mini Gallons Number Seven through Twelve had formed a small row in front of a tablet, watching a channel about ant colonies. They were absolutely riveted. One of them appeared to be taking notes with a tiny pencil.

Buffalo Crush had discovered a channel called "MUKBANG WORLD" and had pulled his chair so close to the screen that his enormous green-glowing eyes were practically touching it. On screen, a human was eating an entire roasted chicken. Buffalo Crush watched with the intensity of a scholar studying ancient texts.

"That," he breathed reverently, "is the most beautiful thing I have ever seen."

"Buffalo, we literally ate three hours ago," said Shadow X from behind him, not looking up from his own screen.

"That was THREE HOURS AGO," Buffalo Crush said, as if this explained everything.

Shadow X had found a channel called "ULTIMATE NINJA FAILS (TRY NOT TO LAUGH)." He was not trying not to laugh. He was, in fact, laughing so hard that his stealth mode kept accidentally activating — one moment he was there, the next he flickered invisible, then reappeared, tears of hydraulic fluid streaming down his violet face.

"THAT GUY TRIED TO THROW A SHURIKEN AND IT BOUNCED OFF A WALL AND HIT HIM IN THE BACK OF THE HEAD—"

He flickered invisible again.

"—AND HE DIDN'T EVEN KNOW AND JUST STOOD THERE—"

*Flicker.*

"—FOR THIRTY SECONDS LOOKING AROUND GOING 'WHERE'S MY SHURIKEN'—"

Shadow X disappeared entirely for a full five seconds, then reappeared wiping his face. "I have watched this clip fourteen times. It gets BETTER every time."

---

In the corner, Blue Cop was standing very straight, arms crossed, watching everyone else with what he clearly intended to be disapproving dignity.

He was not watching YouTube.

He was absolutely not watching YouTube.

He had simply happened to position himself near Buster Gallon's screen. For monitoring purposes. As a law enforcement officer. To ensure no inappropriate content was being accessed on the premises.

The current video was titled: **"SATISFYING POLICE CAR CHASES COMPILATION (WITH SIRENS) 4K."**

Blue Cop's visor flickered blue.

His foot tapped once.

Twice.

"...purely for professional reference," he said to absolutely no one, and then quietly pulled a stool over and sat down.

---

Jun found Mega Trucker in the back of the garage, surrounded by Mini Gallons who had apparently abandoned the ant colony channel to watch along with him. Mega Trucker had found a Latin dance tutorial channel called "SALSA WITH CARLOS" and was currently attempting to follow along.

The problem was that Mega Trucker weighed twelve tons.

"STEP-step-STEP-step-HIP and TURN—" the instructor said cheerfully on screen.

Mega Trucker stepped. The concrete floor cracked. He stepped again. A wrench fell off a shelf. He attempted the hip. Three of Edo's spare engine parts rattled off a workbench.

"MEGA!" Jun yelped, grabbing a falling toolbox. "You're going to bring the whole building down!"

"Jun." Mega Trucker turned to him with absolute sincerity, orange eyes glowing. "Carlos says the secret is *feeling the music in your chassis*."

"Carlos has never MET your chassis!"

"Step three is the cross-body lead." Mega Trucker turned back to the screen eagerly. "I have been practicing for forty-five minutes. Watch."

He stepped. The lights flickered.

"...that's impressive," Jun admitted.

"CARLOS SAYS I HAVE NATURAL RHYTHM!"

---

Phoenix Fire had found a very different kind of channel.

He had started on a cooking channel ("okay, fine, fire is involved in cooking, this is relevant to my interests"), then drifted to a camping channel ("fire safety! educational!"), then somehow arrived at a channel called "WORLD'S BIGGEST BONFIRES AND FIRE SHOWS."

He was watching with his hands clamped over his eyes. Peeking through his fingers. Then covering them again.

"That one's too big," he murmured. "Way too big. Someone could get hurt. Does no one in this video have a fire extinguisher? WHERE ARE THEIR SAFETY PROTOCOLS?"

The video shifted to a spectacular fire dancer spinning enormous flaming torches.

Phoenix Fire let out a small, pained noise.

"That is not regulation form," he said weakly. "That is definitely not—oh. Oh that's actually— no, no, focus, that is DANGEROUS—"

He watched for another forty-five minutes.

When Mega Ambler walked past and asked what he was watching, Phoenix Fire immediately closed the tab with suspicious speed.

"Nothing. Research. Fire suppression techniques. Don't look."

---

Fleta Z had been quiet for an unusually long time.

Everyone found him in the corner, staring at a screen with enormous green eyes, completely motionless. His giant aircraft wings were half-extended. He looked like he had been turned to stone.

On screen: a nature documentary. A golden eagle, soaring over mountains.

"Fleta?" Jun said carefully.

"...It is me," Fleta Z whispered.

"That's an eagle."

"It is *spiritually* me."

"Okay—"

"I am going to learn to fly like that. Without an aircraft mode. Just *soaring*." He spread his wings slightly wider. "I feel awakened."

"You already have wings," Jun pointed out.

"These are *vehicle* wings. That eagle has *soul* wings." Fleta Z tapped his chest slowly. "I need to find my soul wings, Jun."

Jun looked at Mega Ambler. Mega Ambler shrugged with a *he's been like this for an hour* expression.

---

It was Black Hook who caused the first serious problem.

The navy-blue pirate captain had found a channel called "UNBOXING RARE ANTIQUES AND TREASURE" and had watched approximately four hundred videos in a row. He was now standing in the middle of the garage vibrating with barely-contained energy.

"Treasure," he announced to the room. "There is buried treasure. *Everywhere.* This channel has documented forty-seven separate instances of humans discovering valuables in their old buildings, attics, and ocean floors." He paused. "Edo's garage is old."

Everyone looked at Edo's garage.

Edo happened to walk in at that exact moment with a cup of tea, took one look at Black Hook's expression, and said, "Absolutely not."

"I was only going to look in the *foundations—*"

"NOT IN MY GARAGE, HOOK."

"The algorithm," Black Hook said dramatically, pointing at the ceiling, "has *shown* me things, Edo. The algorithm *knows*."

"The algorithm is trying to keep you watching videos for five more hours!"

"...It's working," Buster Gallon admitted from behind his laptop.

---

The breaking point came at hour three.

No one had done anything productive. Edo's garage was now carpeted in Mini Gallons who had organized themselves into viewing clusters by topic of interest. The sound of twelve different YouTube videos playing simultaneously had created a chaotic audio soup of salsa music, police sirens, ninja fail laughter, mukbang eating sounds, and eagle documentary narration.

Wild Guardy arrived from wherever Wild Guardy went when he wasn't being dramatic, took one look at the scene, and said: "What happened to this place?"

"YouTube," Jun said, rubbing his temples.

"What's YouTube?"

Everyone slowly looked at Wild Guardy. Then at each other.

"Don't," Jun said.

Buster Gallon had already opened a new tab.

"Wild Guardy," Buster Gallon said, "have you ever seen a video called *Knight Fails: Medieval Armor Stunts Gone Wrong*?"

Wild Guardy's eyes narrowed. "I have not."

"You'll want to sit down."

"I never sit dow—" Wild Guardy sat down. "...Is that a man in full plate armor trying to do a cartwheel?"

"He thinks he can do it."

"He absolutely cannot."

"Watch."

Twenty seconds later, Wild Guardy made a sound that was somewhere between a gasp and a laugh, quickly covered it with a dignified cough, and said very stiffly, "That was mildly amusing. I shall watch one more."

Three hours later, Wild Guardy was deep in a rabbit hole of equestrian fail videos, occasionally making sounds of extreme offense and saying things like "That is NOT how you hold a lance, that form is an INSULT to chivalry—" before watching the next video with total fascination.

---

It was Anna who finally arrived and rescued everyone.

She walked in, took in the scene — twelve robots, six Mini Gallons still watching ants, one invisible flickering ninja, Mega Trucker doing a slow salsa in the corner, and Wild Guardy in the depths of a spiral muttering about lance technique — and put her hands on her hips.

"Has anyone done *anything* useful in the last four hours?"

Silence.

Buster Gallon slowly raised one hand. "I learned forty-three new ways not to build a bridge."

"That's—" Anna pinched her nose. "Jun. Your homework?"

Jun looked at his backpack. His homework was still untouched. "I was going to do it. I just had to watch *one* more video about—"

"Stop." Anna held up a hand. "I know exactly what happened. One video. One more video. One more after that. And now it's been four hours and you're all still in the same chairs."

Blue Cop straightened up from his police chase compilation. "I was conducting professional—"

"Blue Cop, you have a playlist of *forty-seven videos* open."

Blue Cop was silent for exactly three seconds. "...They were all relevant."

---

Anna turned off the laptop. Then the tablet. Then she unplugged the little speaker streaming salsa music.

Mega Trucker made a small noise of protest.

"Okay, everyone," she said. "Here are the rules. One hour of YouTube. Then you have to actually *do* something with what you learned." She looked at Buster Gallon. "Build something."

"Done."

"Mega Trucker — actually practice the dance. Outside, where you won't crack the floor."

"YES."

"Buffalo Crush — Edo has ingredients in the kitchen. Try making something."

Buffalo Crush stood up so fast his chair fell over backwards. "COOKING CHALLENGE ACCEPTED."

"Shadow X — teach someone an actual skill from those ninja videos."

"I already know the answer to everything that went wrong in all those videos," Shadow X said, cracking his knuckles. "But sure."

Anna turned to Phoenix Fire last. "And you — are you actually afraid of big fires? Because if you are, we could talk about it."

Phoenix Fire opened his mouth. Closed it. "...I was doing *research*."

"Right. Come find me later."

He nodded, very quietly. "...Okay."

---

The next two hours were genuinely chaotic but actually wonderful.

Mega Trucker practiced his cross-body lead in the parking lot with Fleta Z as an extremely confused but strangely graceful partner. ("This is not what eagles do," Fleta Z said. "We're going with it," Mega Trucker replied.)

Buster Gallon, inspired by the engineering fails, spent forty minutes building something that he claimed was a new navigation system and that immediately fell apart when he switched it on. He called it a success ("I now know four additional ways it won't work!").

Buffalo Crush made something in Edo's kitchen that was technically called "dump truck dumpling stew" and should not have been edible but somehow, inexplicably, was the best thing anyone had eaten all week. Even Blue Cop had two bowls.

Shadow X gathered three Mini Gallons and ran them through what he called a "basic stealth curriculum," most of which involved very small obstacle courses. The Mini Gallons were attentive, focused, and completely terrible at it. Shadow X was delighted.

Jun finished his homework. It took forty minutes because he kept thinking about one more video, but he finished it.

---

That evening, Jun sat on the garage steps while Blue Cop stood beside him in the fading light.

"Okay," Jun said. "So maybe four hours straight was too much."

"I have been reviewing my viewing data," Blue Cop said seriously. "I watched forty-seven videos. I learned useful information from approximately... six."

"Which six?"

A pause. "...Ones, three, seven, twelve, thirty-one, and—" another pause — "forty-seven. Which was a compilation of the previous forty-six."

Jun laughed. "So basically, the first six."

"Essentially."

"The rest were just... fun?"

Blue Cop was very quiet for a moment. "Fun," he repeated, carefully, as if testing the word for structural integrity. "...Yes. I believe that is accurate."

Jun grinned at him. "Blue Cop, you're allowed to just watch something because it's fun."

The police bot's visor flickered. "Star Guardians do not traditionally—"

"You're on Earth now."

Another pause. The evening settled around them. From inside the garage came the sound of Mega Trucker humming salsa rhythms and Buffalo Crush loudly narrating his leftovers to himself like a mukbang host.

"...Perhaps," Blue Cop finally said, "two more videos would not be a violation of any regulation."

Jun smiled. "That's the spirit."

---

## The Lesson

**Every tool is only as useful as how you use it.** YouTube — and all the amazing videos on the internet — is genuinely wonderful. There are dance tutorials, engineering documentaries, nature films, cooking classes, and enough eagle footage to spiritually transform any free-spirited flying bot. But a tool that has no limits slowly takes over, and before you know it, four hours have vanished and nothing you planned got done.

The trick isn't avoiding videos entirely. The trick is *the deal you make with yourself before you press play*: how many, how long, and then what? If you learn something, use it. If you're just having fun, that's fine too — but fun has a clock, and the best fun leaves time for everything else.

**Remember:** "One more video" is the most dangerous phrase on the internet. But "one hour, then I go do something" is how you enjoy the best of both worlds — and maybe even end up making the best dumpling stew your garage has ever tasted.
