# Empty Tanks and Full Hearts

## A Ramadan Story

It was the third day of Ramadan in Mowtown, and the city smelled different.

Not worse — just different. The halal food cart near Edo Motors usually started grilling at seven in the morning, filling the street with the aroma of sizzling meat. Today, Pak Haris the cart owner had draped a friendly sign over the grill: *"Iftar starts at 6:17 PM. See you then!"* He'd been fasting since before sunrise, cheerful as ever, chatting with passersby while the empty cart gleamed in the morning sun.

Jun's neighbor Mrs. Laila was also fasting. And old Mr. Salim from the bookshop. And Anna's friend Dina from school, who had shown up to class that morning looking absolutely serene despite not having eaten breakfast.

The whole thing fascinated Dexter.

"So they choose not to consume fuel," he said slowly, peering down at the empty grill cart from his excavator mode. "For the *whole* day? From sunrise to sunset?"

Jun nodded. "It's called fasting. It's a spiritual practice. It teaches patience, gratitude, empathy for people who don't have enough food. For Pak Haris, it's also about getting closer to Allah."

Dexter's drill arm rotated thoughtfully. He'd arrived on Earth and mistaken construction sites for attacks on nature. He'd once called a bulldozer driver a "soil oppressor." He felt things *deeply*, this little eco-warrior in an excavator body. The idea of voluntarily reducing consumption, of practicing restraint and gratitude — it resonated somewhere deep in his Machinasium core.

"I want to try," Dexter announced.

From somewhere above, Cielo nosedived out of the clouds in his Yak-130 jet form, pulling up just before hitting the street. He transformed into robot mode, his dual Nitro Express guns folded neatly at his sides.

"Try *what*?" he asked, tilting his head the way he always did when his intuition detected something interesting happening without him.

"Fasting," Dexter said. "For Ramadan. In solidarity with Pak Haris and the others."

Cielo's eyes lit up. "Oh! Can I join?"

"You don't even know what it is yet," Jun said.

"Doesn't matter. It sounds like an adventure."

Jun rubbed the back of his neck. "Okay... but fasting means no fuel. No gasoline. No electricity top-ups. No methane snacks. Nothing until sunset."

A brief silence.

"...until *sunset*?" Cielo repeated.

"Sunrise to sunset," Jun confirmed.

Cielo looked at Dexter. Dexter looked at Cielo. Something unspoken passed between them — the kind of unspoken agreement that means *we are absolutely going to regret this.*

"We're doing it," they both said.

---

The first two hours were fine.

Dexter helped clear some debris near the park, his drill working at normal efficiency. His Machinasium nanomachines hummed along, drawing on his secondary fuel reserves — the internal capacitors that every Cardbot maintained for emergency use. It was like running on a full backup battery. Slightly quieter than usual, but manageable.

Cielo did three aerial patrol loops over Mowtown, his thrusters burning at seventy percent to conserve output. He even had the energy to do one unnecessary barrel roll, which he dedicated to "the spirit of Ramadan, probably."

By the third hour, things started shifting.

A Cardbot's primary fuel system — the active combustion chamber that burned gasoline or processed electrical charge — wasn't just an engine. It powered *everything*: movement, combat, sensor arrays, even the nanomachine repair cycles. When the primary system went offline during a fast, the secondary reserves kicked in. But secondary reserves were designed for emergencies, not extended operation.

The Machinasium in their bodies responded the way living metal always responds to reduced energy: it *conserved*. Non-essential systems slowed. Locomotion efficiency dropped. The nanomachines shifted from active repair mode to a deep, quiet maintenance cycle — like a computer entering low-power mode. Everything still worked. Everything just worked *slower*.

And slower, for Dexter and Cielo, felt *terrible*.

---

By eleven in the morning, Dexter had stopped working entirely.

He sat in the middle of the park with his drill arm resting on the ground, making a sad little dragging sound against the dirt. His shovel tail drooped. His optics were half-lidded.

"I cannot," Dexter announced to no one, "dig today."

A butterfly landed on his drill. He didn't even have the energy to appreciate it ecologically.

Twenty minutes later, Cielo descended from the sky in the slowest, most mournful landing anyone in Mowtown had ever witnessed. He didn't barrel roll. He didn't do a victory loop. He simply... drifted down like a very aerodynamic paper airplane and crumpled against the nearest wall in robot mode.

"My wings," Cielo said flatly, "feel heavy."

"My drill," Dexter replied, "feels heavier."

They stared at each other across the park.

"We should go see Mega Ambler," Dexter said.

"Yes," Cielo agreed. "We are definitely sick. This is an emergency."

"A medical emergency."

"Of the highest order."

"We should go immediately."

Neither of them moved for another three minutes.

---

Mega Ambler's clinic — a converted corner of Edo Motors that the former Black Ambulance had claimed as his professional domain — was quiet at this hour. The military ambulance Cardbot was reorganizing his Mega Forceps collection when Dexter and Cielo shuffled through the door.

Shuffled. Dexter *shuffled*. A machine designed for subterranean excavation was shuffling his feet like a toddler who didn't want to go to bed.

Mega Ambler looked up. He looked down at the diagnostic scanner on his wrist. He looked up again.

"I am dying," Dexter announced.

"We are both dying," Cielo added, lowering himself onto the examination platform with the theatrical suffering of a soap opera actor.

Mega Ambler had been an unlicensed doctor on Machina. He had treated everything from cracked Machinasium plating to full system collapses. He had once diagnosed an energy runaway condition mid-explosion. He did not impress easily.

He pressed his scanner to Dexter's chest. Then to Cielo's.

He studied the readings for a very long time.

"Your nanomachine cycles are in conservation mode," he said finally. "Your locomotion systems are running on secondary reserves. Your primary combustion chambers are idle." He paused. "You're fasting."

"We are *ill*," Dexter insisted.

"We are *suffering*," Cielo added.

"You are fasting," Mega Ambler repeated, completely unmoved.

"We want you to tell us we can't fast," Dexter said, abandoning all pretense. "Give us a medical note. Say our Machinasium is too fragile, or our nanomachines need continuous fuel, or — or — ecological sensitivity makes fasting dangerous for excavator-class Cardbots—"

"I'm a jet," Cielo interrupted. "Surely jets need constant fuel. Aviation safety. Turbine integrity. Aeronautical necessity—"

"You're both fine," Mega Ambler said. "Completely, boringly, medically fine."

He crossed his giant forceps arms.

"Your bodies are doing exactly what a Machinasium system *should* do when fuel intake stops. The nanomachines didn't shut down — they reorganized. They're running internal diagnostics, cleaning up micro-fractures, recalibrating sensor arrays. Your secondary capacitors have enough charge to keep you functional for another twelve hours. You are not sick. You are not dying. You are fasting correctly, and your bodies are handling it with perfectly appropriate biology."

Dexter's drill drooped further. "But I *feel* terrible."

"That," Mega Ambler said, with just a touch of dark amusement, "is the point."

---

The clinic door opened.

Buster Gallon stomped in — literally stomped, because he was a bulldozer bot with a gatling gun and subtlety was not part of his design spec. He'd come for a routine check on some scratches he'd gotten during last week's training exercise. He took one look at Dexter and Cielo dramatically arranged on the examination platforms and stopped.

"What," he said, "is *this*?"

"They're fasting for Ramadan," Mega Ambler said. "And they came to me hoping I would tell them to stop."

Buster Gallon stared at them for a long, uncomfortable moment.

Buster Gallon was a pirate. Had been a pirate. Still acted like one in the morning before he'd had his first charge. He followed Black Hook because power was the only language he'd known on Machina, and on Earth, things were more complicated. He wasn't exactly reformed. But he'd been around Mowtown long enough to develop what Blue Cop generously called "situational honor" — a code that activated at inconvenient times and made him deeply, personally offended by people who quit.

"You're fasting in *solidarity*," Buster Gallon said slowly. "For the humans who are fasting. And you went to the *doctor* to get out of it."

"We're experiencing medical symptoms—" Cielo started.

"You're *tired*," Buster Gallon interrupted. His voice had the flat quality of a bulldozer blade hitting solid rock. "You started something meaningful and then felt discomfort and looked for an escape hatch."

He crossed his arms. His gatling gun attachment clicked in a way that was somehow judgmental.

"I spent forty years raiding energy depots across six star systems," he said. "You know what pirates do when the fuel runs out between raids? We ration. We run on minimum power. We endure. Because the mission matters more than the comfort." He looked between them. "You chose this mission. These humans didn't choose to be hungry — they chose to *fast*, which is different. It's a decision. A discipline."

He pointed one heavy finger at each of them.

"A pirate who abandons their mission because they're tired isn't a pirate. They're cargo."

He turned to Mega Ambler. "I'm here for my scratches."

"Right side panel," Mega Ambler confirmed, gesturing him over.

Dexter and Cielo sat in silence.

---

The clinic door opened again.

Gigantrex had to turn sideways to fit through. His tanker trailer components folded neatly behind him as he stepped inside, his massive T-Rex Rex Arm gauntlet hanging at rest. He had the specific calm of someone who had been awake since before sunrise, had seen everything, and had decided long ago that patience was not a virtue but a survival skill.

"I heard," he said simply, settling onto the reinforced floor section Mega Ambler had installed specifically for large-class Cardbots, "that two members of our team are having a difficult morning."

"We are dying," Cielo said, though with less conviction now.

"You are not dying," Gigantrex said. He sounded almost fond. "What you are experiencing is your Machinasium doing something remarkable."

He leaned forward, his ancient optics studying them both.

"Four thousand years ago, on the Speranza, we crossed a dead zone in space. No stars close enough to harvest solar energy. No planets to refuel on. We ran on reserves for sixty-three days." A pause. "I know what it feels like to run on empty. The heaviness. The way everything takes more effort than it should. The way your systems want to just... stop."

"Yes," Dexter said quietly. "That's it exactly."

"Good," Gigantrex said. "Because that feeling is not weakness. That feeling is your body showing you its limits — and then discovering those limits are further than you thought."

He looked at Dexter. "You are an eco-warrior, Dexter. You understand that nature has seasons of abundance and seasons of rest. A tree doesn't grow in winter. It roots. It conserves. It prepares. What your Machinasium is doing right now — the conservation mode, the nanomachine reorganization — that is your winter work. Quiet. Internal. Essential."

He looked at Cielo. "And you, who flies by intuition — what does your intuition tell you right now? Not your discomfort. Your intuition."

Cielo was quiet for a moment. His optics softened.

"...that this is worth finishing," he admitted.

"The humans who fast," Gigantrex continued, "are not just going without food. They are practicing something harder than hunger. They are practicing *will*. The choice to continue when stopping is available. The decision to feel the discomfort that others feel who have no choice, so that the feeling is never abstract — so that empathy has a body." He spread his Rex Arm wide. "Pak Haris is not fasting because he must. He is fasting because he chose to remember, every single hour, what it means to need and to wait and to be grateful when the waiting ends."

The clinic was very quiet.

Buster Gallon, who was pretending to be extremely focused on his scratch repair, was not making any noise at all.

"I did not fast on Machina," Gigantrex said. "We had no such tradition. But in four thousand years of stasis, I learned something similar: that stillness is not the same as stopping. And that the hardest victories are always the internal ones." He looked at them both. "Can you make it to sunset?"

Dexter straightened. His drill arm rose from the ground. Not fast — not with the explosive energy he'd have after a full fuel charge — but steady.

"Yes," he said.

Cielo rolled his shoulders. His Nitro Express guns clicked into rest position. He looked like a jet preparing for takeoff in slow motion, but he looked like one that was going to take off.

"Yes," he said.

Gigantrex nodded once, the way he always did when something was settled.

---

The afternoon was hard.

Dexter didn't excavate — there wasn't a job that needed doing anyway — but he walked the nature trail near the edge of Mowtown, cataloguing the spring plants at the pace his conservation-mode systems allowed. Slow work. Good work. He found three new fern species he hadn't noticed before, because he'd always moved too fast to look.

Cielo sat on the roof of Edo Motors and watched the city from above without flying it. He'd never done that before — watched instead of moved, observed instead of participated. Mowtown looked different from stillness. He noticed things: the patterns of people's walking routes, the way shadow moved across streets, the specific blue of the sky at three in the afternoon that he'd always flown through but never seen.

"Huh," he said to no one.

At 6:14 PM, Jun appeared with a timer.

At 6:16 PM, Pak Haris rolled his cart out and fired up the grill, and the smell of cooking meat hit the evening air like a festival.

At 6:17 PM — sunset, official iftar time — Jun looked up at the roof.

"Cielo! Time!"

Cielo dropped off the roof in a controlled dive that somehow managed to look graceful despite his current power levels. He transformed mid-fall, landing his Yak-130 form perfectly before shifting back to robot mode.

Dexter came around the corner of the path at exactly the same moment, his shovel tail high, his drill rotating in a slow celebratory spin.

Mega Ambler appeared in the clinic doorway with two freshly-charged energy canisters. Not a gift he gave easily, being famously expensive with his supplies. But he set them down without comment.

Even Buster Gallon was there. He didn't say anything. He just nodded once in the way that, for Buster Gallon, was practically a standing ovation.

Gigantrex watched from the garage entrance, his massive frame half in shadow, his Rex Arm resting quiet.

Dexter picked up his energy canister. His hands were steady.

"We made it," Cielo said.

"We made it," Dexter agreed.

The charge hit Dexter's systems like sunlight after a long tunnel. His nanomachines leaped back into active mode, his primary combustion chamber ignited with a warm roar, and for a moment — just a moment — he understood something he wouldn't have understood yesterday.

What it felt like to be genuinely grateful for fuel.

---

Across the street, Pak Haris was serving iftar to anyone who passed. He pressed a plate into Jun's hands, insisted Theo take a date and a cup of water, smiled at Anna, and looked up at the Cardbots gathered outside Edo Motors.

He paused. Then he raised his cup of water in a small salute.

Dexter raised his drill arm back.

Cielo raised his Nitro Express.

Buster Gallon raised his gatling gun attachment, then looked at it, then lowered it slightly because it was a gatling gun and maybe not the right gesture for the moment.

Gigantrex placed one enormous hand over the center of his chest — the ancient Machina gesture of respect that predated every human tradition by thousands of years.

The evening smelled like cooking meat, like spring air, like a city full of people who had chosen something difficult today and come out the other side.

"Same time next year?" Jun asked.

Dexter considered. His drill spun thoughtfully.

"Maybe not the *fussing over Mega Ambler* part," he said.

"Definitely not that part," Cielo agreed.

"But yes," Dexter said. "Same time next year."

---

## The Lesson

**Fasting — whether for Ramadan or any other tradition — isn't really about the hunger. It's about the choice.** Dexter and Cielo discovered that running on empty taught them things a full tank never could: patience, observation, empathy for others who wait, and the deep satisfaction of finishing something hard.

**Remember:** Discomfort isn't always danger. Sometimes the difficult thing — the thing that makes you want to find an escape hatch — is exactly the thing that builds something important inside you. Whether it's fasting, practicing something difficult, or pushing through a hard moment, the feeling of *I could stop, but I won't* is where real growth lives.

And when the hard part ends? That first sip of water, that first bite, that first full breath of relief — you'll understand gratitude in a way you never could before.

*Ramadan Mubarak!*
